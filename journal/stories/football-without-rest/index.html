Football’s Bigger Calendar Has a Smaller Margin for Rest

More global competition creates more spectacle. It also moves the cost of football’s growth onto players’ bodies.

By APGO Sport · 20 July 2026 · 7 min read
In this pieceExpansionRest is structuralWhat to measureBetter design

Football’s calendar is often described as if it were weather: congested, unfortunate and nobody’s responsibility. It is better understood as a design. Every tournament, commercial tour, international window and domestic competition occupies time that cannot be used twice. When organizers add a new event, they are not adding days to the year. They are deciding whose recovery will shrink.

The first 32-team FIFA Club World Cup made that design visible. Played in the United States from 15 June to 13 July 2025, it placed eight groups and a four-round knockout path into a month that had previously offered many elite players some combination of recovery, national-team duty and preseason. FIFA said the schedule would guarantee a minimum of three days between matches. That standard addresses the space between kickoffs; it does not by itself create an off-season.

The formatEight groups of four, the top two in each group advancing, then a single-match knockout competition from the round of 16 to the final. A finalist could play seven tournament matches.

Expansion is a distribution question

There is a good argument for a more global club competition. Clubs outside Europe deserve meaningful matches, attention and revenue. Supporters benefit when football’s continents meet in games that matter. The mistake is to frame the choice as global inclusion versus player welfare. A credible calendar has to deliver both.

Who gains from an extra competition is visible: organizers sell rights, clubs receive prize money, sponsors gain inventory and broadcasters fill schedules. Who pays is more diffuse. Players absorb travel and reduced recovery. Domestic leagues adjust dates. National teams compete for availability. Supporters are asked to treat every event as essential.

A match can meet the minimum rest rule and still belong to a season with no meaningful rest.

Rest is not the gap between two games

Three days between matches is not the same thing as a protected off-season. Recovery includes sleep, travel, training load, minor injury management and a period away from competition. A player finishing one club tournament, joining a national team, returning for preseason and beginning a domestic campaign may satisfy a series of local rules while experiencing one continuous year.

FIFPRO’s 2025 men’s workload report projected that Achraf Hakimi could reach 74 matches and nearly 7,000 minutes across club and national-team obligations. The importance of that figure is not that one player defines the whole sport. It is that modern elite football can produce workloads that are difficult to see if every organizer counts only its own competition.

The calendar also creates inequality inside squads. Starters accumulate minutes and high-intensity travel. Marginal players may train continuously but receive too little meaningful competition. Women’s football often faces a different but related problem: some players are overloaded at the top while others lack stable professional minutes. “More football” is not a single condition.

Count exposure, not just appearances

A serious public workload measure should combine match minutes, consecutive starts, travel, climate, rest days and the number of transitions between club and national-team environments. It should distinguish a substitute appearance from 120 minutes in a knockout match. It should also show uncertainty when travel or training data are unavailable.

This matters for ranking players. A performance index that rewards total output without accounting for opportunity and exposure can accidentally celebrate overload. Availability belongs in an evaluation model, but so does the context in which availability was demanded.

A better calendar needs a budget

The sport needs the equivalent of a workload budget: protected minimum off-season time, limits on consecutive high-exposure periods and a transparent accounting of who is already carrying the heaviest load. New competitions should state how they fit inside that budget before they are approved.

That would not settle every dispute. Clubs, players, leagues and federations will still disagree about money and sporting priorities. But it would move the debate from slogans to trade-offs. A calendar should not be judged only by whether every match can be scheduled. It should be judged by whether the people playing those matches can recover enough to make them worth watching.
