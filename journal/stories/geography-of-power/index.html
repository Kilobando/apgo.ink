The New Geography of Power Runs Through the Sea

Shipping lanes, undersea cables, chokepoints and sovereignty are redrawing the map of global influence.

By APGO Desk · 20 July 2026 · 9 min read
In this piece One map Chokepoints The cable layer What resilience means

For most readers, the sea appears as empty space between places that matter. For governments, shipping companies and network operators, it is closer to a crowded operating system. The same corridors carry fuel, food, manufactured goods and the physical cables that make a supposedly wireless economy work. When a strait narrows or a route becomes dangerous, the effects do not remain at the waterline.

The map of power is therefore becoming maritime again—not because land has become irrelevant, but because the systems that connect land are unusually concentrated at sea. A disruption can begin as a security event, a drought, a damaged cable or a regulatory decision. It then travels through insurance, freight prices, delivery times, cloud access and public budgets.

The scale UN Trade and Development says maritime transport carries more than 80% of world merchandise trade by volume. The International Telecommunication Union says submarine cables carry more than 99% of intercontinental data traffic.

Goods and information now share one strategic map

The two systems are not identical. A container vessel cannot be rerouted like a packet, and a cable failure does not behave like a closed canal. But both networks depend on a limited set of favorable routes, landing points, ports, repair capacity and political permissions. That dependence turns geography into leverage.

UNCTAD’s 2026 note on global maritime transport describes overlapping pressure in the Red Sea and Suez Canal, the Black Sea and, at different moments, the Panama Canal and Strait of Hormuz. The point is not simply that several places are risky. It is that disruption in one corridor changes demand and congestion elsewhere. Detours consume more fuel and ship capacity; arrivals bunch at ports; freight contracts reprice uncertainty.

The sea is not the gap between economies. It is the infrastructure inside the global economy.

A chokepoint exports its costs

In 2024, seaborne trade volumes grew by 2.2%, while ton-miles—the volume of cargo multiplied by the distance it travels—grew by 5.9%. UNCTAD attributes much of that difference to longer routing around the Cape of Good Hope as Red Sea disruption persisted. The world moved somewhat more cargo, but worked much harder to move it.

By early May 2025, tonnage transiting the Suez Canal remained 70% below its 2023 average, according to the same UNCTAD review. The consequences were not distributed evenly. Countries with fewer transport options, weaker port connections or higher import dependence absorbed a larger share of the delay and price shock. “Resilience” can sound like a technical property; in practice it often means who can afford redundancy.

That is why a narrow maritime passage can be a geopolitical instrument without ever being formally closed. Risk alone changes behavior. Insurers raise premiums, operators reroute, governments issue guidance and markets price the possibility of interruption. Control is only one form of power. The ability to make a route uncertain is another.

Below the shipping lanes, the cable layer

Submarine telecommunications cables add a less visible layer. The ITU describes more than 1.7 million kilometres of commercial cable supporting digital connectivity. Around 200 faults occur in a typical year, and most are caused by ordinary physical activity such as fishing and anchoring rather than sabotage. Yet the strategic concern is real: countries dependent on one landing point or a small number of routes can face severe disruption when repair access is slow.

The Red Sea demonstrates how the two networks overlap. It is a central shipping route and an important cable corridor. When security constraints complicate access, the same waters can affect physical trade, data redundancy and the ability of repair vessels to reach damaged infrastructure. The vulnerability is not a cinematic image of a single cut cable. It is a system with too few alternatives and too much delay.

Resilience is a political choice

The obvious response is redundancy: more routes, more landing points, better-connected ports and sufficient repair vessels. But redundant infrastructure is expensive, crosses jurisdictions and can take years to approve. It also requires states and private operators to share information while protecting commercially and nationally sensitive systems.

A serious maritime policy therefore has to join areas that governments often separate. Trade ministries need to understand cable resilience. Digital ministries need to understand ports and repair access. Climate adaptation belongs in the same conversation as security, because drought, storms and changing water levels can stress routes without an adversary making a move.

The lesson is not that every cable break or shipping delay is a geopolitical plot. It is almost the reverse. Systems can be strategically important while failing for ordinary reasons. Professional policy begins by seeing both at once: the dramatic possibility of coercion, and the mundane reality of maintenance.
