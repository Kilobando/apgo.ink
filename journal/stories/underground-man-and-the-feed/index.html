Dostoevsky’s Underground Man Was Built for the Feed

A spiteful, self-conscious narrator from 1864 explains more about posting than most theories of social media.

By APGO Reading Room · 20 July 2026 · 8 min read
In this essayAn imaginary audienceToo consciousFreedom to refuseWhy read it now

The Underground Man has no account, no followers and no glowing screen. He still knows the emotional weather of the feed. He writes toward an audience he claims to despise, anticipates every objection, turns humiliation into performance and keeps speaking long after silence would protect him. His monologue is not a post, but it has the velocity of one.

Fyodor Dostoevsky published Notes from Underground in 1864. Its unnamed narrator is a retired civil servant living apart from society in St. Petersburg. He is intelligent, isolated, resentful and exhausted by his own intelligence. He does not merely remember an insult; he rehearses the insult, imagines the other person’s version, attacks that version and then attacks himself for caring.

The bookNotes from Underground appeared in 1864. The public-domain Constance Garnett translation is available free through Project Gutenberg.

An audience assembled from objections

The narrator repeatedly addresses “gentlemen” who may not exist. They are less characters than a pressure inside his sentences. He predicts what they will say, replies before they can speak and treats disagreement as proof that his performance has found its target. The imaginary audience makes solitude interactive.

Online speech often works the same way. A person begins with an observation and quickly finds themselves answering a hostile reply that has not arrived. The imagined critic determines the tone. Irony accumulates as armor. The writer seems to address everyone while really conducting a private trial.

The Underground Man does not need an algorithm to trap him. Anticipation is enough.

This is why his voice feels more modern than a simple prophecy about technology. Dostoevsky is not predicting software. He is studying a habit of consciousness that software can reward: the conversion of every feeling into a reply, every embarrassment into a case, every case into a performance of superiority.

The status game of being too conscious

The Underground Man distinguishes himself through excess awareness. Other people can act because they are simple. He cannot act because he sees every motive, counter-motive and possible humiliation. His paralysis becomes evidence of refinement. If he suffers more, perhaps it is because he understands more.

This is a familiar status move online. Detachment can appear smarter than commitment; diagnosing the incentive behind an action can substitute for evaluating the action itself. The person who can explain why everyone is performing may be performing too, but with the added prestige of appearing outside the game.

Dostoevsky’s narrator knows this. He exposes his own vanity and then uses the confession as another claim to superiority. You cannot unmask him because he has already described the mask. Self-awareness does not free him. It makes the room harder to leave.

The freedom to refuse what is good

The book’s philosophical argument concerns rational schemes of human flourishing. If people truly understood their interests, the theory goes, they would choose what benefits them. The Underground Man insists that a person may reject advantage simply to prove that choice remains theirs. He treats irrationality as a final territory of freedom.

The feed offers endless small versions of that rebellion. A user knows the argument is unproductive and sends it anyway. Someone destroys a relationship to escape the feeling of being managed. Another chooses the interpretation that causes the most pain because accepting a kinder one would feel like surrender. Harm can be experienced as authorship.

This does not make the narrator heroic. His freedom is cramped and reactive. He can still choose, but mostly chooses against. The tragedy is not that he lacks agency. It is that resentment supplies the menu.

Why read him instead of diagnosing him

It would be easy to turn the Underground Man into a clinical label or a social-media type. The book resists that comfort. His cruelties are specific, yet the machinery behind them is widely recognizable: the wish to be seen without becoming vulnerable, the need to win an encounter already lost, the fantasy that exposing one’s worst motive cancels responsibility for it.

Literature is useful here because it can remain inside a contradiction longer than a slogan. Dostoevsky does not ask us to approve of the narrator or reduce him to a warning. He gives resentment a voice precise enough to become embarrassing. We recognize the trap from within.

The best reason to read Notes from Underground now is not that it “predicted the internet.” It is that it shows how a human being can build a feed out of memory, pride and an imaginary crowd. Technology scales the room. The room was already there.
